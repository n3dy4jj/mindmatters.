Question 1 — Outcome PD5.2

What is Anxiety & How Common Is It?

Anxiety is a mental health condition characterised by persistent, excessive worry, fear, or nervousness that is disproportionate to the situation and significantly interferes with daily life. Unlike normal feelings of stress or nerves, clinical anxiety does not go away once the trigger is removed, and often has no clear external cause. It includes a spectrum of disorders — generalised anxiety disorder (GAD), social anxiety disorder, panic disorder, and phobia-related disorders.

Anxiety is the most common mental health condition in Australia. According to Beyond Blue, anxiety affects approximately 1 in 4 Australians at some point in their lifetime. For young people aged 15–24, it is particularly prevalent: data from the Australian Institute of Health and Welfare (AIHW) indicates that 26% of young Australians in this age group experience anxiety-related disorders — making it the leading mental health issue in this demographic.

Research from headspace (2023) indicates that anxiety symptoms are increasing among young Australians, with school pressure, social media, and economic uncertainty identified as key contributing factors. Significantly, many young people do not seek help — with the average time between symptom onset and treatment being 11 years, highlighting the urgent need for awareness and destigmatisation.

Sources: Beyond Blue (2023); AIHW Mental Health Report (2022); headspace National Youth Mental Health Survey (2023)

Question 2 — Outcome PD5.2

How Anxiety Impacts Young People's Lives

Anxiety doesn't just affect how you feel in the moment — it can disrupt nearly every area of a young person's life. Here are two key ways it creates lasting impact.

```
📚

Impact 1: Academic & Educational Disruption

One of the most significant impacts of anxiety on young people is its interference with academic performance and educational participation. Anxiety triggers the body's fight-or-flight response, flooding the brain with cortisol — a stress hormone that impairs concentration, memory retention, and problem-solving ability. For students, this means difficulty focusing during class, blanking during exams (known as test anxiety), and struggling to complete assignments, even when they are academically capable.

Beyond cognitive effects, anxiety frequently leads to school avoidance. Young people may refuse to attend school due to social anxiety, fear of performance-based situations such as presentations, or generalised dread. According to headspace (2023), approximately 1 in 3 young Australians with anxiety report that it has significantly disrupted their education — leading to missed school days, incomplete assessments, and long-term effects on career pathways. The flow-on consequences — reduced academic achievement, lower self-esteem, and limited future opportunities — make this one of the most serious impacts of the condition.

Source: headspace National Youth Mental Health Survey, 2023; Black Dog Institute, 2022

Impact 2: Social Isolation & Relationship Difficulties

Anxiety profoundly impacts a young person's social life and relationships — two of the most important aspects of wellbeing during adolescence and early adulthood. Social anxiety disorder, which affects approximately 7% of young Australians, causes intense fear of social situations, leading individuals to believe they will be judged, humiliated, or rejected by others. Even for those with generalised anxiety, the constant state of internal tension makes socialising exhausting and overwhelming.

This can cause young people to withdraw from friendships, avoid social events, and decline opportunities such as sports, extracurricular activities, or part-time work. The resulting isolation creates a damaging cycle: social withdrawal reduces opportunities for positive social experiences, which in turn worsens anxiety and contributes to low self-worth. Research from the Black Dog Institute (2022) demonstrates that young people experiencing social anxiety are significantly more likely to develop comorbid depression, as prolonged loneliness and disconnection erode emotional wellbeing and a sense of belonging — both critical protective factors for mental health.

Source: Black Dog Institute (2022); Australian Bureau of Statistics Mental Health Survey (2021)

Question 3 — Outcomes PD5.1 & PD5.7

Two Ways Young People Can Manage Anxiety

```
01
Evidence-Based Strategy

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT)

Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) is widely regarded as the gold standard treatment for anxiety disorders. It is a structured, evidence-based form of psychotherapy that helps young people identify and challenge the unhelpful thought patterns driving their anxiety — then gradually replace them with more realistic and balanced thinking.

CBT operates on the principle that our thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are interconnected. When a young person with anxiety catastrophises (e.g., "if I say something wrong, everyone will hate me"), CBT helps them recognise this cognitive distortion, examine the evidence, and form a more grounded response. A core component is gradual exposure — slowly facing feared situations in a safe, controlled way — which reduces avoidance behaviour and builds confidence over time.

Research from the Australian Psychological Society (2021) shows CBT produces significant reductions in anxiety symptoms in 60–80% of young people who complete a course of treatment. Many GPs can provide a Mental Health Care Plan which covers bulk-billed CBT sessions, making it accessible for young Australians.

  • 1Identify anxious thoughts and triggers
  • 2Challenge and reframe unhelpful thinking
  • 3Gradually face feared situations (exposure)
  • 4Build coping skills and resilience over time
Source: Australian Psychological Society (2021); headspace (2023)
02
Lifestyle Management

Mindfulness & Regular Physical Activity

Two of the most powerful and accessible strategies young people can use daily are mindfulness meditation and regular physical activity. While each is effective individually, combining them produces particularly strong results for managing anxiety.

Mindfulness involves intentionally focusing attention on the present moment — breathing, sensations, and surroundings — without judgement. This directly counteracts anxiety, which thrives on "what if" thinking about future scenarios. Apps like Smiling Mind (developed in Australia) provide free, evidence-based mindfulness programs designed specifically for young people. Studies show that even 10 minutes of mindfulness per day can meaningfully reduce anxiety symptoms within 8 weeks.

Physical activity reduces anxiety through multiple biological pathways: it lowers cortisol (the stress hormone), releases endorphins (natural mood elevators), and improves sleep quality — itself a critical factor in managing anxiety. The Australian Government's Physical Activity Guidelines recommend 60 minutes of moderate-to-vigorous exercise daily for young people, and adherence has been consistently linked with lower rates of anxiety and depression.

  • 1Try 10 min of mindfulness each morning (use Smiling Mind app)
  • 2Aim for 60 min of physical activity daily (sport, walking, dancing)
  • 3Limit screen time & prioritise 8–10 hours sleep
  • 4Build a consistent routine — predictability reduces anxiety
Source: Australian Government Dept. of Health (2021); Smiling Mind Research Report (2022)
